The following is a summary based on the official announcement by the LIGO Scientific Collaboration and Virgo Collaboration, reflecting key information about the first confirmed observation of gravitational waves:

Detection Date: September 14, 2015. This is the actual date when the gravitational wave signals were observed by LIGO's twin observatories.

Detection Source: Two merging black holes, each about 30 times the mass of our sun.
Signal Source Distance: 1.3 billion light-years. This distance information is crucial for time alignment as it defines the timescale of signal propagation.
Detection Sites: LIGO twin observatories in Livingston, Louisiana, and Hanford, Washington.
Time Difference: The signal first reached Livingston, then propagated at the speed of light, reaching Hanford seven milliseconds later.
Scientific Significance: The detection results closely match the predictions of Einstein's general theory of relativity and provide the first direct evidence that black holes merge.
This historic detection not only confirmed the existence of gravitational waves but also provided us with a precise cosmic "timestamp" that can be used for potential cross-civilization time alignment.
