Earlier that year, the Future of Life Institute’s Puerto Rico conference (Jan 2015) issued an open letter (co-signed by hundreds of AI researchers) calling for “robust and beneficial” AI, reflecting rising concern about AGI safety in the research community (first inklings of formal AGI safety efforts). Also in 2015, Britain’s **DeepMind** was acquired by Google, which agreed to set up an AI Ethics Board – an early sign of governance considerations around powerful AI.

- **2016** – *AI beats humans in Go; policy groundwork.* Google DeepMind’s **AlphaGo** program defeated world Go champion Lee Sedol in March 2016, a landmark achievement in AI ([Google DeepMind CEO says that humans have just over 5 years ...](https://fortune.com/2025/03/18/google-deepmind-ceo-demis-hassabis-agi-outsmart-human-workers-job-replacement-ai/#:~:text=,next%20five%20to%2010)). Go had been considered a decade away from being solved by AI; AlphaGo’s victory demonstrated the power of deep learning and reinforcement learning, and was viewed by some as a step toward more general problem-solving AI. This event raised public awareness of AI’s rapid progress. In the policy realm, the Obama White House released reports on AI’s future (October 2016), noting the long-term possibility of AGI and urging preparatory research into societal impacts. **(Achieved)**

- **2017** – *Transformer revolution; national AI strategies.* The breakthrough deep learning architecture **Transformer** was introduced in 2017 (“Attention is All You Need”), enabling much larger and more general language models in subsequent years. This technical innovation is a backbone of current AGI-esque systems. In September 2017, Russian President **Vladimir Putin** declared that *“Artificial intelligence is the future…whoever becomes the leader in this sphere will become the ruler of the world.”* ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=%E2%80%9CArtificial%20intelligence%20is%20the%20future%2C,%E2%80%9D)), underscoring global strategic stakes. That same year, **China’s State Council issued the New Generation AI Development Plan**, setting a goal for China to become the **world’s primary AI innovation center by 2030** ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=The%20development%20of%20artificial%20intelligence,basic%20science%20and%20technology%20research)) ([Full Translation: China’s ‘New Generation Artificial Intelligence Development Plan’ (2017)](https://digichina.stanford.edu/work/full-translation-chinas-new-generation-artificial-intelligence-development-plan-2017/#:~:text=Third%2C%20by%202030%2C%20China%E2%80%99s%20AI,foundation%20for%20becoming%20a%20leading)). This plan led to massive investments in AI R&D in China and is often cited as the start of the “AI arms race” between the U.S. and China. **(Achieved policy)** 

- **2018** – *GPT and the first AGI lab charter.* OpenAI released the first **GPT-1** language model (June 2018) and more famously **GPT-2** (Feb 2019, research preprint in 2018) which demonstrated unprecedented text generation ability. Notably, OpenAI initially withheld the full GPT-2 model over misuse concerns, sparking debate about responsible disclosure ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=The%20development%20of%20artificial%20intelligence,basic%20science%20and%20technology%20research)). In 2018, OpenAI also published its **Charter**, explicitly stating its commitment to AGI and a policy to cooperate or merge with a project that achieves AGI before them to avoid duplication and safety issues. Meanwhile, DeepMind researchers published work on **“Reward is Enough”**, positing that a generally capable intelligence could emerge from reinforcement learning alone – a philosophical milestone for AGI research. **(Achieved)**

- **2019** – *Turing tests and new benchmarks; corporate moves.* The term “**AGI**” gained more public attention. In early 2019, the AI research community proposed new benchmarks explicitly aiming to test general problem-solving: François Chollet introduced the **ARC (Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus)** to evaluate a system’s ability to solve novel tasks without prior training (a proxy for general intelligence) ([ARC Prize Survives 3 Months](https://arcprize.org/blog/3-month-update#:~:text=ARC%20Prize%20Survives%203%20Months,unbeaten%20as%20of%20September%202024)). This became the basis for the later ARC Prize. No AI came close to human-level on ARC in 2019 ([ARC Prize Survives 3 Months](https://arcprize.org/blog/3-month-update#:~:text=ARC%20Prize%20Survives%203%20Months,unbeaten%20as%20of%20September%202024)). On the industry side, OpenAI transitioned from non-profit to a capped-profit hybrid model (March 2019) to attract capital for the expensive compute needed for AGI, raising a $1 billion investment from Microsoft. **(Achieved)**. Also in 2019, reinforcement learning agents mastered complex games: **OpenAI’s Dota2 bot** defeated world champions, and DeepMind’s **AlphaStar** achieved Grandmaster level at StarCraft II – significant because these games are real-time and partially observed, closer to real-world complexity than board games. Each breakthrough was touted as progress toward more general AI. Policy note: In late 2019, the **European Commission released its AI ethics guidelines**, one of the first governmental frameworks referencing the need to keep AI under human control even as it advances (foreshadowing the AI Act).

- **2020** – *Scaling up: GPT-3 and beyond.* OpenAI unveiled **GPT-3** in June 2020, a 175-billion parameter language model that stunned observers with its few-shot learning abilities – it could perform tasks it wasn’t explicitly trained for just by being given examples in the prompt ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=The%20development%20of%20artificial%20intelligence,basic%20science%20and%20technology%20research)). GPT-3’s versatility (from coding to writing essays) was arguably the first instance of a single AI system showing broad competence across domains, albeit still with significant limitations. Many viewed GPT-3 as a step closer to AGI, or at least an “AGI precursor.” Around the same time, DeepMind’s work on **protein folding AI (AlphaFold2)** showed an AI system solving a grand challenge of biology – by late 2020, AlphaFold2 achieved such high accuracy in protein structure prediction that it was essentially solving a 50-year scientific problem ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=The%20development%20of%20artificial%20intelligence,basic%20science%20and%20technology%20research)). This highlighted AI’s power in scientific reasoning, a key aspect of general intelligence. **(Achieved)**. On policy: The U.S. enacted the National AI Initiative Act (2020), coordinating government efforts on AI; it didn’t directly regulate AI but invested in AI research and track progress, implicitly acknowledging strategic importance.

- **2021** – *Multi-modality and strategic competition.* In 2021, AI systems became more **multi-modal**: OpenAI’s **DALL-E** (Jan 2021) and **CLIP** demonstrated AI that connects language and vision, able to create images from text or understand image content. Such cross-domain ability is a component of general intelligence. OpenAI also released **Codex** (summer 2021), an AI model that could translate natural language to code, later powering GitHub’s Copilot – hinting that AI could generalize to programming tasks (a critical test for AGI). In terms of benchmarks, OpenAI’s models began tackling the **ARC** challenge, but performance remained modest; ARC was still essentially unsolved by any AI in 2021 (Chollet estimated AI was far from the human level of ~80% on ARC at that time). Key policy events underscored AGI as a geopolitical issue: In March 2021, the U.S. **National Security Commission on AI (NSCAI)**, a government-appointed body, warned that the U.S. was **not prepared for the coming AI era and that China could surpass the U.S.** if action was not taken ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=The%20development%20of%20artificial%20intelligence,basic%20science%20and%20technology%20research)). NSCAI’s final report urged massive investment in AI, calling AI superiority vital for national security, and even referenced the need to win the “technology competition” akin to a new Sputnik moment. **(Achieved)**. Additionally, in September 2021, the EU proposed the **AI Act**, a comprehensive regulatory framework for AI, which included provisions to regulate high-risk AI systems and discussed future “general AI” under oversight – this was the first major attempt to legally framework AI development at a continental scale. *(Projection: EU law was still under negotiation in 2021; it would be adopted in 2024.)*

- **2022** – *Explosion of generative AI; governance initiatives.* The year 2022 saw rapid advances in generative AI that captured global attention. **OpenAI’s ChatGPT** launched publicly in November 2022 and gained 100 million users in ~2 months – the fastest adoption of any consumer application at that time. ChatGPT (based on GPT-3.5) familiarized hundreds of millions with AI that can converse, solve problems, and draft texts at a near-human level, intensifying discussions of AGI in society ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=Since%20OpenAI%27s%20ChatGPT%20chatbot%20,outdo%20each%20other%27s%20AI%20accomplishments)). Earlier in 2022, OpenAI’s **DALL-E 2** and the open-source **Stable Diffusion** (Aug 2022) enabled image generation from text, showing creative potential. Technical milestone: **DeepMind’s Gato** (2022) was showcased as a single agent with over 600 tasks (from playing Atari games to captioning images to controlling a robot arm) – *an explicit effort at a generalist system*, though its performance per task was modest ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=The%20development%20of%20artificial%20intelligence,basic%20science%20and%20technology%20research)). By late 2022, leading AI models began to approach or exceed human-level on specific academic benchmarks (for instance, some language models passed standard tests like SAT questions, and coding models solved easy LeetCode problems). On the policy side, important **governance actions** emerged: the U.S. Department of Commerce in October 2022 issued **export controls on advanced AI chips to China**, seeking to slow China’s progress in training frontier models ([Updated October 7 Semiconductor Export Controls](https://www.csis.org/analysis/updated-october-7-semiconductor-export-controls#:~:text=On%20October%207%2C%202022%2C%20the,function%20and%20where%20they%20aim)). These controls, which restricted chips like NVIDIA A100/H100 for China ([Updated October 7 Semiconductor Export Controls](https://www.csis.org/analysis/updated-october-7-semiconductor-export-controls#:~:text=performance%20density%20as%20a%20parameter,may%20require%20prior%20notification%20and)), explicitly cited AI and national security concerns. China, for its part, invested heavily in domestic AI chip projects to reduce reliance on U.S. tech. Another notable event: In September 2022, the White House OSTP released a “**Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights**,” outlining principles for safe and ethical AI (transparency, fairness, accountability) – while not law, it signaled growing U.S. government attention to advanced AI impacts. **(Achieved)** 

- **2023 (H1)** – *Frontier model breakthroughs and first global policy responses.* The first half of 2023 was marked by **GPT-4’s release (March 2023)**, which represented a significant leap in general capability. GPT-4 achieved human-level scores on many professional exams and showed emergent abilities like solving novel math puzzles, coding complex programs, and understanding images (via a multimodal version) ([Governance of superintelligence | OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/governance-of-superintelligence/#:~:text=Given%20the%20picture%20as%20we,one%20of%20today%E2%80%99s%20largest%20corporations)). A Microsoft research paper titled “**Sparks of AGI**” argued that GPT-4 exhibits glimmers of general intelligence ([Governance of superintelligence | OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/governance-of-superintelligence/#:~:text=Given%20the%20picture%20as%20we,one%20of%20today%E2%80%99s%20largest%20corporations)). This reignited debate on how close we are to AGI. Around the same time, **Anthropic launched Claude**, Google introduced its **PaLM 2** model and the Bard chatbot, and numerous startups (Character.AI, etc.) rolled out advanced systems – the competition in frontier AI heated up. In March 2023, an open letter from the Future of Life Institute – signed by Elon Musk and many others – **called for a 6-month pause** in training AI more powerful than GPT-4, citing existential risk ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=,the%20need%20to%20take%20action)). This letter (an extraordinary public plea) showed the level of fear even within the tech community regarding uncontrolled AGI development. Although the pause didn’t occur, it spurred discussion and some self-regulatory moves. In May 2023, OpenAI CEO Sam Altman testified to the U.S. Congress, urging AI regulation and suggesting the formation of a federal agency to license advanced AI models ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=Lawmakers%20and%20AI%20companies%20are,technology%20and%20asking%20for%20regulation)). Policymakers started comparing the situation to the nuclear era, calling advanced AI a “genie” that cannot be put back but must be managed ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=Lawmakers%20and%20AI%20companies%20are,technology%20and%20asking%20for%20regulation)). **(Achieved)**. In June 2023, the UK announced it would host a **Global Summit on AI Safety** to bring nations together – reflecting an emerging global governance push. Also mid-2023, leading AI labs including OpenAI, Google, Anthropic **voluntarily pledged new AI safety measures** in an agreement announced by the White House ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=,the%20need%20to%20take%20action)). These included external red-team testing of models and watermarking AI-generated content. These were *precursor actions* as formal regulation was still pending.

- **2023 (H2)** – *Regulatory milestones and multi-national cooperation.* In July 2023, the **EU Parliament approved the draft AI Act**, moving it closer to law (it would later be finalized in 2024). This law included special rules for “foundation models” and generative AI, requiring disclosure of training data and risk management for very powerful models. In August 2023, **China implemented the Interim Measures for Generative AI** ([China: Generative AI Measures Finalized  | Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2023-07-18/china-generative-ai-measures-finalized/#:~:text=On%20July%2010%2C%202023%2C%20the,solicit%20comments%20from%20the%20public)) ([China: Generative AI Measures Finalized  | Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2023-07-18/china-generative-ai-measures-finalized/#:~:text=Article%206%20of%20the%20draft,the%20%2020%20Algorithmic%20Recommendation)) – regulations requiring providers of generative AI services (like ChatGPT-like models in China) to **register with the government and adhere to content censorship and security reviews**. These rules, effective Aug 15, 2023, were the first national-level generative AI rules in the world ([China: Generative AI Measures Finalized  | Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2023-07-18/china-generative-ai-measures-finalized/#:~:text=On%20July%2010%2C%202023%2C%20the,solicit%20comments%20from%20the%20public)) ([China: Generative AI Measures Finalized  | Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2023-07-18/china-generative-ai-measures-finalized/#:~:text=Article%206%20of%20the%20draft,the%20%2020%20Algorithmic%20Recommendation)). Meanwhile, model capabilities kept rising: by late 2023, **GPT-4 and Claude were being augmented with tool use and memory**, forming early agentic systems (e.g. AutoGPT and other AI “agents” that could loop on tasks). In October 2023, the **U.S. President issued an Executive Order on Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy AI**, mandating that developers of foundation models above a certain capability threshold notify the government and share safety test results ([Governance of superintelligence | OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/governance-of-superintelligence/#:~:text=First%2C%20we%20need%20some%20degree,a%20certain%20rate%20per%20year)). It also directed the NIST to set standards for AI testing. This was the most concrete U.S. government action on AI to date, treating frontier AI as a matter of national security (the order invoked the Defense Production Act for the first time for AI). **(Achieved)**. November 2023 saw the **UK’s Bletchley Park AI Safety Summit**, where 28 countries (including the US, China, EU states) signed the **Bletchley Declaration** acknowledging the risks from **frontier AI** (systems more powerful than today’s) and vowing to collaborate on evaluation and monitoring ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=On%20February%2011%2C%20heads%20of,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground)) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=What%20this%20has%20resulted%20in,and%20rooted%20in%20shared%20prosperity)). It established an ongoing process for international discussions on AI safety. Around the same time, a dramatic incident at OpenAI occurred: CEO Sam Altman was briefly fired by his board, reportedly due to tensions over the pace of AGI development and disagreements about **AI safety and disclosure** ([OpenAI researchers warned board of AI breakthrough ahead of CEO ouster, sources say | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/sam-altmans-ouster-openai-was-precipitated-by-letter-board-about-ai-breakthrough-2023-11-22/#:~:text=the%20board%20leading%20to%20Altman%27s,respond%20to%20requests%20for%20comment)) ([OpenAI researchers warned board of AI breakthrough ahead of CEO ouster, sources say | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/sam-altmans-ouster-openai-was-precipitated-by-letter-board-about-ai-breakthrough-2023-11-22/#:~:text=Some%20at%20OpenAI%20believe%20Q,in%20most%20economically%20valuable%20tasks)). He was reinstated after employee revolt, but the episode highlighted the internal debate on how quickly to push toward AGI. **(Achieved)**. In December 2023, OpenAI’s prototype model “**o3**” was reported to score **87.5% on the ARC benchmark**, effectively solving many of its abstract tasks ([Daniel Franzen & Jan Disselhoff - ARC Prize 2024 winners](https://creators.spotify.com/pod/profile/machinelearningstreettalk/episodes/Daniel-Franzen--Jan-Disselhoff---ARC-Prize-2024-winners-e2upr5k#:~:text=the%20ARC%20Prize%202024,they%20revealed%20how%20they)) – a breakthrough for general reasoning (the ARC Prize remained unclaimed until these results). **(Achieved)**.

- **2024** – *Progress and projections.* By 2024, many experts believed that **artificial general intelligence was within reach within a decade or less**, based on trend extrapolation. In early 2024, labs continued releasing improved models: e.g., OpenAI’s GPT-4 Turbo (with vision and longer context) and **Anthropic’s Claude 2.1** with 100k+ token context. In February 2024, a second **Global AI Summit** was held in Seoul, building on the Bletchley Park process (South Korea and others stressing *AI for good* and *alignment*). Notably, the **United States and UK did not sign the communiqué** at the Paris “AI Action Summit” in February 2025, indicating some divergence in approach by late 2024/early 2025 ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=On%20February%2011%2C%20heads%20of,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground)) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=What%20this%20has%20resulted%20in,and%20rooted%20in%20shared%20prosperity)) (this foreshadowed the rise of a more multilateral, Global-South-inclusive approach in 2025). Technologically, **GPT-4.5** (an internal OpenAI upgrade) and **Google’s Gemini 1.0** were rumored in 2024, but the biggest confirmed leap came in late 2024 when Google DeepMind began testing **Gemini 2** series with remarkable results on internal evaluations (these results became public with Gemini 2.5 Pro in 2025). In July 2024, the **EU AI Act was officially adopted**, making the EU the first region to pass a comprehensive AI law ([Long awaited EU AI Act becomes law after publication in the EU's ...](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/long-awaited-eu-ai-act-becomes-law-after-publication-eus-official-journal#:~:text=Long%20awaited%20EU%20AI%20Act,will%20commence%20on%202)). It set compliance deadlines in 2025–2026 for companies to meet requirements on transparency and risk mitigation for advanced AI. **(Achieved policy)**. Also in 2024, the United Nations convened an **AI Advisory Body** to advise on global governance (UN Secretary-General called for the creation of a global AI watchdog akin to the IAEA). Meanwhile, OpenAI announced in late 2024 plans to develop **“Superalignment”** – a dedicated project with $100 million funding to solve aligning superintelligent AI within four years, implicitly *assuming they might create such an AI by 2028* (a public projection) ([Superintelligence: OpenAI Says We Have 10 Years to Prepare](https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/superintelligence-openai-says-we#:~:text=Superintelligence%3A%20OpenAI%20Says%20We%20Have,Governance%20of%20superintelligence)). This was a clear declaration that they see the timeline to AGI/superintelligence as very short. **(Projection)**.

- **2025 (Q1)** – *Latest developments (as of April 2025).* In the first quarter of 2025, **Google DeepMind released Gemini 2.5 Pro** (March 2025), which immediately **shattered records on many AGI-oriented benchmarks** ([Google Unveils Gemini 2.5 Pro, Shattering Records on Humanity's Last Exam | Beebom](https://beebom.com/google-unveils-gemini-2-5-pro-shattering-records-on-humanity-last-exam/#:~:text=math%2C%20and%20science)) – a tangible milestone toward more general AI. For instance, its 18.8% score on **Humanity’s Last Exam (HLE)** was the highest to date on that extremely challenging test of cross-domain knowledge ([Google Unveils Gemini 2.5 Pro, Shattering Records on Humanity's Last Exam | Beebom](https://beebom.com/google-unveils-gemini-2-5-pro-shattering-records-on-humanity-last-exam/#:~:text=,Google%27s%20AI%20Studio%20for%20free)). In early April 2025, Google DeepMind published “An Approach to Technical AGI Safety,” its most detailed public plan for managing risks from AGI, which openly stated *AGI might be here in “the coming years” and could pose existential risks if misaligned* ([Google DeepMind releases paper on AGI safety](https://blog.google/technology/google-deepmind/agi-safety-paper/#:~:text=Artificial%20general%20intelligence%20,that%20it%20is%20developed%20responsibly)) ([AI Could Achieve Human-Like Intelligence By 2030 And 'Destroy Mankind', Google Predicts](https://www.ndtv.com/science/ai-could-achieve-human-like-intelligence-by-2030-and-destroy-mankind-google-predicts-8105066#:~:text=,clear%20examples%20of%20severe%20harm)). This marks a historic point where a leading AI lab acknowledges both the near-term possibility of human-level AI and the need for special global precautions. Policy-wise, February 2025 saw the **third global summit (Paris “AI Action Summit”)** where notably India and France led a coalition pushing for inclusive AI governance and open research, reflecting increasing involvement from the Global South ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=What%20this%20has%20resulted%20in,and%20rooted%20in%20shared%20prosperity)) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=centers%20toward%20a%20multi,and%20rooted%20in%20shared%20prosperity)). By this time, *some discord among the major Western powers* had appeared (with the U.S. taking a go-it-alone stance in some respects, emphasizing leadership and its own safeguards over international commitments ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=On%20February%2011%2C%20heads%20of,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground)) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=year,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground))). **(Achieved)**. In March 2025, the United States introduced draft legislation to establish a **Federal AI Safety Commissioner**, indicating regulatory structures catching up. **Demis Hassabis (DeepMind) projected in March 2025** that *“some form of AGI” is likely within a few years (5–10 years)* ([Human-level AI will be here in 5 to 10 years, DeepMind CEO says](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/03/17/human-level-ai-will-be-here-in-5-to-10-years-deepmind-ceo-says.html#:~:text=Human,next%20five%20or%2010%20years)), aligning with OpenAI’s Sam Altman who had said in 2023 it’s plausible in *~6 years* ([Superintelligence: OpenAI Says We Have 10 Years to Prepare](https://www.thealgorithmicbridge.com/p/superintelligence-openai-says-we#:~:text=Superintelligence%3A%20OpenAI%20Says%20We%20Have,Governance%20of%20superintelligence)). These projections are now on record by leaders of top labs. By April 2025, **no self-aware or autonomous “strong AI” exists**, but the consensus in public statements is that **the timeline to AGI is possibly very short**, and every passing month brings new breakthroughs. This timeline demonstrates a rapid convergence of **technological capability milestones** (especially 2023–2025) with **governance milestones** (global summits, regulations), highlighting that AGI is no longer a distant speculation but a present planning consideration for societies. *(All above items are documented via public sources; future projections are italicized and attributed accordingly.)*

## 3. Global Societal and Emotional Responses to AGI Development

The pursuit of AGI and superintelligent AI has elicited a complex mix of hope, excitement, fear, and skepticism across different societies. Public opinion varies by region and demographic, and it has evolved significantly from 2015 to 2025 as AI capabilities became more visible. Below we review the **global societal response**, drawing on reputable surveys (Pew Research, Ipsos, YouGov, etc.), academic studies, and think-tank reports. We break down attitudes by region – the United States, Europe, China/Asia, and the Global South – and note demographic patterns (age, gender, profession) where data is available. All findings reflect the state of knowledge as of early 2025.

### United States

In the United States, public sentiment toward advanced AI and potential AGI is characterized by a dominant sense of **concern, coupled with cautious interest**. According to a **2023 Pew Research Center survey**, **52% of Americans said they are more concerned than excited** about the increasing use of AI in daily life, versus only 10% who are more excited than concerned ([What the data says about Americans' views of artificial intelligence](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2023/11/21/what-the-data-says-about-americans-views-of-artificial-intelligence/#:~:text=intelligence%20www.pewresearch.org%20%2052,are%20more%20excited%20than%20concerned)). This marked an increase in worry compared to a similar survey in 2021 ([Pew Research: Americans Feel More Concerned Than Excited ...](https://campustechnology.com/articles/2023/09/20/pew-research-americans-feel-more-concerned-than-excited-about-ai.aspx#:~:text=Pew%20Research%3A%20Americans%20Feel%20More,increase%20of%2015%20percentage)), indicating that as AI (like ChatGPT) became more known, public wariness grew. A **majority of Americans (approximately two-thirds)** fear that AI could have negative effects: a May 2023 Reuters/Ipsos poll found **61% of U.S. adults believe AI poses a threat to humanity’s future** – essentially agreeing that **AI could “threaten civilization”** if mismanaged ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=May%2017%20%28Reuters%29%20,Reuters%2FIpsos%20poll%20published%20on%20Wednesday)) ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=More%20than%20two,believe%20it%20could%20threaten%20civilization)). This high level of concern was roughly triple the share who did *not* foresee such risks ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=The%20Reuters%2FIpsos%20poll%20found%20that,number%20of%20those%20who%20don%27t)). Notably, this survey also revealed partisan and ideological nuances: **70% of 2020 Trump voters feared AI could threaten humankind, compared to 60% of Biden voters** ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=Those%20who%20voted%20for%20Donald,higher%20levels%20of%20concern%3B%2070)). Similarly, Americans with strong religious backgrounds (e.g. evangelical Christians) voiced above-average concern that AI might run amok ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=When%20it%20came%20to%20religious,Christians%20were%20more%20likely%20to)). These findings suggest a broad-based apprehension, cutting across political and demographic lines but with conservatives and some religious groups especially distrustful of AI’s trajectory.

Despite fears, Americans also acknowledge potential benefits of AI, leading to a nuanced emotional mix. Pew found many Americans simultaneously **marvel at AI’s capabilities** – for example, being impressed by ChatGPT’s ability to generate human-like answers – yet **distrust its use** by institutions. According to Pew’s late 2023 study comparing the public and AI researchers, only **18% of U.S. adults trust technology companies to use AI in a responsible way** ([What the public thinks about AI and the implications for governance](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/what-the-public-thinks-about-ai-and-the-implications-for-governance/#:~:text=governance%20www,Similarly%2C%20in%20a%20May)). This aligns with an **Edelman Trust Barometer (2023)** finding that tech sector trust had declined, with “AI companies” seen as driven by profit over public interest. 

When it comes to **jobs and the economy**, American attitudes are split by exposure. Pew (2023) reported that many U.S. workers hadn’t yet felt direct threat from AI – only about **13% of workers said they are very concerned their job will be replaced** ([AI is making the world more nervous | Ipsos](https://www.ipsos.com/en-us/ai-making-world-more-nervous#:~:text=One%20in%20three%20workers%20expect,loss%20of%20their%20current%20job)) – but expectations are pessimistic: Gallup and Ipsos polls in 2023 show roughly **3 in 4 Americans expect AI will eliminate more jobs than it creates in society**. The phrase “AI anxiety” entered popular media to describe white-collar professionals worrying that advanced GPT-type systems could displace roles in law, finance, or software. Indeed, surveys find **younger American workers (Millennials/Gen Z)**, who are more tech-savvy, are both more optimistic about leveraging AI in their careers *and* more anxious about long-term job security in an AI-driven economy ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Among%20workers%2C%2057,with%20differences%20of)) ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Again%2C%20optimism%20about%20AI,older%20or%20have%20no%20college)). This seeming contradiction reflects a *hope vs. fear* ambivalence: younger people see personal opportunities with AI tools but fear macro-level job competition.

Emotionally, terms often used in U.S. survey comments include **“fascination,” “awe,” but also “dread”** and “helplessness.” A 2022 YouGov poll asked Americans to choose words describing their feelings on AI: the top picks were “cautious” (54%) and “concerned” (47%), vastly outweighing “enthusiastic” (only ~18%) ([Americans are increasingly skeptical about AI's effects | YouGov](https://today.yougov.com/technology/articles/51803-americans-increasingly-skeptical-about-ai-artificial-intelligence-effects-poll#:~:text=Americans%20are%20increasingly%20skeptical%20about,descriptions%20as%20apply%20to%20them)). High-profile events also swung emotions: for example, when ChatGPT’s abilities went viral in late 2022, excitement spiked; but following open letters about existential risk in 2023, **fear and calls for slowing down AI gained mainstream traction** (covered extensively on TV news). By 2025, it’s fair to say **American society is more fearful than hopeful about AGI in the near term**, with a majority believing benefits will eventually come, but after significant disruption ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=May%2017%20%28Reuters%29%20,Reuters%2FIpsos%20poll%20published%20on%20Wednesday)) ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=More%20than%20two,believe%20it%20could%20threaten%20civilization)). Still, a substantial minority – often those more educated or in tech fields – express **excitement about AGI’s potential** (for curing diseases, boosting productivity, etc.), tempered by calls for strong safety measures.

### Europe

In **Europe**, public attitudes toward AGI and advanced AI mirror those in the U.S. in many ways, with an overall cautious stance, but there are some regional distinctions. European publics generally report **lower excitement and higher skepticism** about AI’s benefits compared to global averages ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Excitement%20is%20highest%20in,all%20predominantly%20Anglophone%20countries%20and)) ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Trust%20in%20AI%20varies,that%20companies%20using%20AI%20will)). For instance, the **2023 Ipsos Global Views on AI survey** found **excitement about AI was lowest in Europe and North America**, whereas emerging markets showed more enthusiasm ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Excitement%20is%20highest%20in,all%20predominantly%20Anglophone%20countries%20and)). Only around half (or less) of Europeans agreed that AI products have more benefits than drawbacks ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Globally%2C%20only%20about%20half,are%20nervous)). Countries like **France and Germany consistently show wariness**: A 2023 YouGov poll indicated France had one of the highest levels of concern in Europe, with **49% “worried” about generative AI** versus only ~30% not worried ([Understanding global attitudes towards generative AI](https://business.yougov.com/content/46944-understanding-global-attitudes-towards-generative-ai#:~:text=Europe%20presents%20a%20similar%20mixed,respectively)). Similarly, in Germany and the UK, surveys show more people think AI will make life worse rather than better – a **2022 European Tech Insights report** recorded that over 60% of EU respondents favored slowing down AI development to manage risks.

European skepticism can be partly attributed to cultural and policy context: EU citizens have strong privacy norms and often distrust big tech. A 2024 European Commission special barometer reported a majority in EU countries support strict regulation on AI, even if it slows innovation, reflecting a precautionary principle mindset. Emotive responses in Europe include **“distrust”** – not only of the technology but of **governments and companies handling AI**. According to the **2024 Reuters Institute survey**, Europeans worry about misuse of AI in media (deepfakes, misinformation), which makes them anxious about AI’s societal impact ([What does the public in six countries think of generative AI in news?](https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/what-does-public-six-countries-think-generative-ai-news#:~:text=What%20does%20the%20public%20in,in%20the%20next%20five%20years)). For example, more than **70% of Europeans surveyed in late 2023 believed AI would increase the spread of disinformation online** ([Artificial Intelligence: Key insights, data and tables - Ipsos](https://www.ipsos.com/en-us/artificial-intelligence-key-insights-data-and-tables#:~:text=Ipsos%20www,think%20it)), fueling a sense of *distrust*.

That said, Europe also has pockets of optimism: countries in Northern Europe (e.g. Sweden, Denmark) showed relatively lower concern in the YouGov generative AI poll – only **33% in Sweden were worried** (the lowest in the survey) ([Understanding global attitudes towards generative AI](https://business.yougov.com/content/46944-understanding-global-attitudes-towards-generative-ai#:~:text=Europe%20presents%20a%20similar%20mixed,respectively)), and a non-trivial segment in Scandinavia and the Netherlands see AI as an opportunity to improve efficiency in public services (some EU nations have experimented with AI in governance transparently). **Younger Europeans** (under 35) tend to be more optimistic about tech and more open to AI’s benefits, similar to global trends ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=America%3B%20it%20is%20also%20higher,Japan%2C%20Korea%20and%20Eastern%20Europe)). Education also plays a role: college-educated Europeans report feeling more informed and slightly more positive about AI innovations than those without college education ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=America%3B%20it%20is%20also%20higher,Japan%2C%20Korea%20and%20Eastern%20Europe)). However, even among youth, climate change and economic issues often rank higher than AI on the list of concerns, so AI fear, while present, competes with other anxieties.

An interesting observation from surveys: **awareness levels in Europe** – in 2023 about **1 in 10 people in Britain and Denmark said they were *unaware* of generative AI** entirely ([Understanding global attitudes towards generative AI](https://business.yougov.com/content/46944-understanding-global-attitudes-towards-generative-ai#:~:text=The%20highest%20concern%20is%20found,respectively)), indicating a portion of the public not yet engaged emotionally simply due to lack of exposure. As ChatGPT and similar tools become integrated (Bing, etc.), this portion is shrinking. By 2025, most Europeans have heard of these tools; the debate has mainstreamed with EU’s AI Act coverage. The emotional tone in European media often emphasizes *ethics and human rights*, leading to cautious or negative framing. For example, French and German media frequently run stories on AI threatening jobs or privacy, feeding public concern. Thus, Europe’s societal response can be summarized as **one of measured concern and demand for control**: people are *less swept up in hype*, more focused on potential downsides (somewhat more so than Americans), but not panicking – rather, channeling worry into calls for strong governance. Hope is present largely in terms of **AI solving problems (medicine, climate)**, but with the stipulation “if properly regulated.” Surveys by European institutions in 2023 showed a majority agreeing that *“AI can be good for society, *if* we manage it right.”* 

### China and East Asia

In **China**, the societal response to AI and prospects of AGI has been relatively more **optimistic and enthusiastic**, according to available polling and reports, though it’s tempered by concerns about economic disruption. Chinese public discourse (as filtered through state media and online platforms) often emphasizes AI as a driver of progress and national strength, in line with government messaging. Surveys indicate **higher trust in AI among Chinese citizens** compared to Western counterparts. For example, the **Ipsos 2023 global survey** showed that **excitement about AI was highest in emerging Asian economies**, and nervousness was lowest in East Asia (with **Japan, South Korea, and China** reporting less AI anxiety than Anglophone countries) ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Nervousness%20is%20highest%20in,markets%20and%20among%20people%20under)) ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Excitement%20is%20highest%20in,all%20predominantly%20Anglophone%20countries%20and)). Specifically, a 2023 YouGov poll across 18 countries found that in China **about 51% said they are *not worried* about generative AI** (versus ~30-35% worried) ([Understanding global attitudes towards generative AI](https://business.yougov.com/content/46944-understanding-global-attitudes-towards-generative-ai#:~:text=Among%20the%20Asian%20countries%20surveyed%2C,respectively)) – a stark contrast to Western nations where the majority were worried. Hong Kong showed similar results, with 55% not worried ([Understanding global attitudes towards generative AI](https://business.yougov.com/content/46944-understanding-global-attitudes-towards-generative-ai#:~:text=Among%20the%20Asian%20countries%20surveyed%2C,respectively)). This suggests a more positive baseline attitude.

The **Chinese public’s hopefulness** about AI often revolves around economic benefits. Many Chinese see AI as an opportunity for the country to lead technologically; a 2020 Chinese survey by Tencent Research found over 70% of respondents believed AI will greatly improve society and daily life. **Trust in government handling** is also higher: The public generally assumes the government will guide AI development responsibly (which may lower personal fear). However, this optimism coexists with **job displacement fears**, as China has a large workforce potentially automatable. A 2022 survey by a Chinese university indicated that a majority of Chinese urban workers expected **some jobs would be lost to AI/robots**, but they also believed *new* jobs would emerge. The **level of fear of AI “destroying humanity”** appears lower in Chinese discourse; existential risk is not a prominent theme in state media. Instead, concerns focus on things like AI causing social inequality or AI being used maliciously by other countries (security framing). 

**Demographically**, younger Chinese (Gen Z, Millennials) are extremely tech-savvy and seem quite positive about AI. They grew up with rapidly improving technology and often express excitement about AI in products (voice assistants, etc.). In contrast, **older generations in China** (who did not grow up with the internet) show more caution and confusion about AI, though they defer to government reassurances. It’s important to note that direct polling on “AGI” specifically is rare in China – much of the data is about AI generally. But qualitative evidence (like Chinese social media discussions) suggests emotions like **national pride** (“China will win the AI race” sentiment) and **curiosity** about advanced AI systems (ChatGPT was a trending topic, with many Chinese netizens finding ways to use it despite restrictions, treating it as a fascinating new tool). When the Chinese government temporarily restricted access to ChatGPT in early 2023, it was reportedly due to content control, but many citizens sought to try it – indicating *enthusiasm to engage* with frontier AI.

In **East Asia outside China**, attitudes vary: **Japan** tends to have a somewhat *techno-optimist yet pragmatic outlook*. The Ipsos data showed Japan had the lowest reported “nervousness” about AI ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Nervousness%20is%20highest%20in,lowest%20in%20Japan%2C%20Korea%20and)) – possibly reflecting a culture that has long imagined coexistence with robots (a trope in Japanese media). Japanese industry and government promote AI to cope with issues like aging society, and public surveys in Japan show relatively high acceptance of robotics and AI in assisting roles. However, Japan is also cautious about AGI uncontrolled growth – its strategy is pro-innovation but with ethical guidelines (e.g. Japan joined the G7’s Hiroshima AI Process in 2023). **South Korea** is very bullish on AI; South Korean public surveys indicate a strong belief that AI will boost the economy, and excitement is high (Korea topped some indices of AI readiness optimism). Yet Koreans also report personal nervousness about privacy and job impacts, a mix of tech enthusiasm and risk awareness.

Overall in Asia, one can say **there is more optimism and less doomsday fear** about AGI compared to the West ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Excitement%20is%20highest%20in,all%20predominantly%20Anglophone%20countries%20and)) ([Understanding global attitudes towards generative AI](https://business.yougov.com/content/46944-understanding-global-attitudes-towards-generative-ai#:~:text=Among%20the%20Asian%20countries%20surveyed%2C,respectively)). Regions like **India and Southeast Asia** show *both* high excitement and high worry – for instance, YouGov found India and Indonesia had the **highest percentages “very worried”** about AI (57–59%) ([Understanding global attitudes towards generative AI](https://business.yougov.com/content/46944-understanding-global-attitudes-towards-generative-ai#:~:text=worried%E2%80%99%20about%20it%20%2839,consumer%20sentiment%20varies%20by%20market)) *and* a strong belief that AI will make life better. This could be due to lower baseline trust in governance (hence worry) combined with a desire for technological progress (hence optimism). **China** specifically, due to its government narrative and controlled media environment, has a public that is **generally supportive** of AI development and less publicly fearful of AGI apocalypses, focusing instead on tangible concerns like jobs and geopolitical competition. Emotions in China: *optimism, pride, and a bit of anxiety about economic adjustments*.

### Global South and Other Regions

In the **Global South** (broadly, Latin America, Africa, Middle East, and parts of Asia-Pacific not already discussed), perspectives on AGI are influenced by developmental priorities and existing economic challenges. Data from global surveys suggests that **emerging economies are often more optimistic about AI’s long-term benefits** than developed countries. For example, **Ipsos 2023** finds optimism about AI’s impact is *much higher in the Global South* – regions like **Latin America, the Middle East, and Africa have majorities believing AI will improve their lives and society**, often seeing it as a leapfrog technology ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=emerging%20markets%20and%20lowest%20in,all%20predominantly%20Anglophone%20countries%20and)) ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Again%2C%20optimism%20about%20AI,older%20or%20have%20no%20college)). In Nigeria, Brazil, India, etc., many respondents agreed that AI will “significantly change daily life in the next 3-5 years” and were excited for it ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Globally%2C%20only%20about%20half,are%20nervous)) ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=countries.%20%E2%80%A2%20On%20average%2C%2066,in)). This optimism ties to hopes that AI could, for instance, improve healthcare access, education, or government services in resource-constrained settings.

However, the Global South is not without **fears and suspicions**. A recurring sentiment is **fear of being left behind** or **exploited** by AGI developments dominated by the Global North. Thought leaders in Africa and South Asia have raised concerns that if AGI is achieved by a few big companies/countries, poorer nations might have little say in its use, possibly exacerbating inequality. There’s also the fear of job loss, perhaps even more acutely felt in countries with large young workforces (e.g. India’s IT sector worries about automation by AI). An international **2023 Ipsos poll** noted that on average **36% of workers globally expect AI to replace their current job**; this expectation was *highest in Southeast Asia* and other developing regions, where up to ~50% fear displacement ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Among%20workers%2C%2057,Asia%20and%20lowest%20in%20Northern)). In contrast, in Northern Europe only ~12-15% thought likewise ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=replace%20their%20current%20job,those%20who%20are%20younger%20and%2For)). So, job anxiety is significant in the Global South, even as people are hopeful about new opportunities AI might bring.

**Latin America:** Public opinion data on AI is sparser, but available surveys (like Latinobarómetro or IPSOS local polls) show Latin Americans are concerned about issues like misinformation and automation. For instance, a 2023 survey in Brazil found a majority worried about AI spreading fake news (given the region’s experience with WhatsApp rumors). Yet governments in LatAm, such as Colombia or Uruguay, often emphasize AI for social good, and populations generally welcome tech that can tackle corruption or crime. Emotional response: cautious optimism, with a historical wariness due to past technological inequalities.

**Africa:** There is a notable optimism among African youths about technology; many see AI as a tool for leapfrogging development. A 2022 AI Media survey in Africa found that over 60% of respondents across several African countries felt AI would **increase productivity and economic growth**. However, awareness of AGI specifically is low among the general public; the conversation is more among tech communities. African commentators emphasize **inclusion** – the emotional tone is hopeful but also *determined* that Africa should not miss the “next big thing” as it did during prior industrial revolutions ([The Rise of AI in the Global South and the Need for Inclusion](https://www.wilsoncenter.org/blog-post/rise-ai-global-south-and-need-inclusion#:~:text=In%20the%20context%20of%20the,to%20understand%20the%20inputs)). Some African AI researchers have written about “AI colonialism” – fearing that AGI could further centralize power. So, there is a mix of excitement for its potential to solve problems and guardedness about fair access.

**Middle East:** Gulf countries (like UAE, Saudi Arabia) are highly bullish on AI – governments invest heavily and citizens often echo optimism about diversifying economy through AI. In contrast, conflict-torn regions worry more about misuse (e.g. surveillance, autonomous weapons). Emotional responses vary from *ambition* (e.g., UAE aiming to be an AI hub) to *fear* (in places where AI might be used oppressively). A 2021 Arab Youth Survey noted that while many Arab young people welcome tech innovations, they also fear unemployment from automation.

**Demographic divides:** Globally, certain patterns emerge regardless of region. **Younger people (under 35)** are consistently more open to AI and confident they can adapt; **older people** show more fear of the unknown and less understanding of AI (leading to either mistrust or dismissal). **Gender differences** have been observed: men tend to express more optimism about AI’s benefits and their personal ability to use AI, whereas women often report slightly higher concern about negative impacts ([Public Opinion | The 2023 AI Index Report | Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2023-ai-index-report/public-opinion#:~:text=Public%20Opinion%20,companies%20that%20use%20AI%2C)). For example, an Ipsos 2022 survey found **men were more likely than women to say AI makes their lives easier and to trust AI-powered products** ([Public Opinion | The 2023 AI Index Report | Stanford HAI](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2023-ai-index-report/public-opinion#:~:text=Public%20Opinion%20,companies%20that%20use%20AI%2C)). This may relate to tech industry gender gaps and differences in experience. **Education level** strongly correlates with attitudes: those with higher education are more aware of what AI can do and more optimistic about managing it (while also aware of risks), whereas less-educated populations sometimes fear AI due to lack of understanding or fear of job competition in manual labor.

**Emotional and ideological divides:** Across many countries, there’s an emerging ideological split: those with a more **techno-libertarian or techno-optimist** mindset (often in tech hubs, higher socio-economic status) feel excitement and a sense of inevitability about AGI – they focus on ensuring *their* country or group benefits. On the flip side, people with a **humanitarian or egalitarian worldview** often express concern about AGI exacerbating inequalities or being dangerous without global regulation – this group includes many academics, civil society leaders, etc., who call for caution (sometimes labeled as AI “doomers” in media, though their views are nuanced). These camps exist within regions; e.g. in the U.S. some Silicon Valley figures hype AGI’s promise while many activists and sociologists voice fears of bias and surveillance.

To summarize global sentiment as of 2025: **Hope and excitement exist, especially around AI’s ability to solve big problems and improve lives, but they are often matched or outweighed by fear and distrust**. In numerical terms, surveys often find pluralities or small majorities are **worried rather than optimistic** about AI’s overall impact ([What the data says about Americans' views of artificial intelligence](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2023/11/21/what-the-data-says-about-americans-views-of-artificial-intelligence/#:~:text=intelligence%20www.pewresearch.org%20%2052,are%20more%20excited%20than%20concerned)) ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=May%2017%20%28Reuters%29%20,Reuters%2FIpsos%20poll%20published%20on%20Wednesday)). However, this general worry coexists with **a recognition of AI’s inevitability** – few think we can or should stop development entirely; instead they desire it to be well-governed. There is also a psychological aspect: some individuals report a kind of *resignation* (“the AI revolution is coming whether we like it or not”) which can amplify anxiety or, conversely, lead to a detached acceptance. By region, **North America and Europe** lean more negative/concerned, **Asia (especially China)** leans more positive/trusting, and the **Global South** leans hopeful but insistent on not being marginalized ([Global Advisor - Global views on A.I. 2023](https://www.ipsos.com/sites/default/files/ct/news/documents/2023-07/Ipsos%20Global%20AI%202023%20Report-WEB_0.pdf#:~:text=%E2%80%A2%20Again%2C%20optimism%20about%20AI,older%20or%20have%20no%20college)). These attitudes are fluid and can rapidly shift with events – for example, a major benign success (like AI curing a disease) could swing sentiment positive, while an AI-related disaster or misuse could spur panic. As of April 2025, global society is in a state of *watchful uncertainty*, emotionally charged by the sense that AGI might soon change the world, for better or worse.

## 4. Global Environment for AI Development: Geopolitics and Policy

The development of AGI and advanced AI is unfolding in a complex **global geopolitical environment**, where competition and cooperation coexist. Different regions have distinct policy priorities and strategic approaches to AI. This section assesses the current global landscape: the U.S.-China dynamic, Europe’s regulatory leadership, and the engagement of the Global South, as well as multilateral efforts. As of 2025, **AI is seen as a critical element of national power** ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=Russian%20president%20Vladimir%20Putin%20has,come%20to%20dominate%20global%20affairs)), and this drives both competitive behavior (a race to lead in AI) and collaborative initiatives (to set norms and avoid catastrophe). We will outline each major region and then look at international cooperation.

### United States: Competition and Safe Innovation

The **United States** views leadership in AI (and by extension any future AGI) as a national security and economic priority. U.S. policy circles often echo the sentiment that *“whoever leads in AI will rule the world,”* a phrase famously highlighted by Putin ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=%E2%80%9CArtificial%20intelligence%20is%20the%20future%2C,%E2%80%9D)) and taken seriously in Washington. The U.S. government’s stance, especially since 2018, is that **America must win the global competition for AI supremacy** to maintain its military and economic edge ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=The%20development%20of%20artificial%20intelligence,basic%20science%20and%20technology%20research)). This has led to significant federal investments: e.g., the Pentagon established the Joint Artificial Intelligence Center (JAIC) in 2018, the NSF launched AI research institutes, and DARPA ran a multi-year “AI Next” program. In 2021, the NSCAI (mentioned earlier) explicitly framed AI leadership in terms of competing with China ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=The%20development%20of%20artificial%20intelligence,basic%20science%20and%20technology%20research)). So geopolitically, the U.S. sees China as its primary rival in AI.

One manifestation is the **export control regime** the U.S. introduced: In October 2022, the U.S. **banned the export of cutting-edge AI chips to China** without a license ([Updated October 7 Semiconductor Export Controls](https://www.csis.org/analysis/updated-october-7-semiconductor-export-controls#:~:text=On%20October%207%2C%202022%2C%20the,function%20and%20where%20they%20aim)) ([Updated October 7 Semiconductor Export Controls](https://www.csis.org/analysis/updated-october-7-semiconductor-export-controls#:~:text=performance%20density%20as%20a%20parameter,may%20require%20prior%20notification%20and)). This was expanded in 2023 to close loopholes ([Updated October 7 Semiconductor Export Controls](https://www.csis.org/analysis/updated-october-7-semiconductor-export-controls#:~:text=Bureau%20of%20Industry%20and%20Security,function%20and%20where%20they%20aim)) ([Updated October 7 Semiconductor Export Controls](https://www.csis.org/analysis/updated-october-7-semiconductor-export-controls#:~:text=Computing%2FSupercomputing%20Interim%20Final%20Rule%20www,style)). The goal is to slow China’s ability to train the largest-scale models (since modern AGI-aspiring models require thousands of top-end GPUs like NVIDIA A100/H100). These controls represent a significant geopolitical lever, effectively trying to **handicap Chinese AI labs**. The U.S. has also pressured allies (Netherlands, Japan) to restrict semiconductor equipment to China, aiming to constrain China’s capacity to produce its own advanced chips ([Updated October 7 Semiconductor Export Controls - CSIS](https://www.csis.org/analysis/updated-october-7-semiconductor-export-controls#:~:text=On%20October%207%2C%202022%2C%20the,restraining%20Chinese%20military%20modernization%20efforts)) ([AI Chips for China Face Additional US Restrictions - The Diplomat](https://thediplomat.com/2024/04/ai-chips-for-china-face-additional-us-restrictions/#:~:text=AI%20Chips%20for%20China%20Face,to%20advanced%20AI%20chips)). From China’s perspective, this is a major front in the tech Cold War (and has spurred efforts for self-reliance, see below). The U.S. justifies these as necessary to “restrain military AI development” in China ([Updated October 7 Semiconductor Export Controls](https://www.csis.org/analysis/updated-october-7-semiconductor-export-controls#:~:text=On%20October%207%2C%202022%2C%20the,function%20and%20where%20they%20aim)), but it clearly has broader strategic intent.

Domestically, the U.S. environment is a mix of **free-market innovation and emerging regulation**. The U.S. tech industry (OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, etc.) leads in cutting-edge AI models, largely unfettered by government restrictions so far. The federal government has not imposed direct limits on training advanced models (unlike the proposed pause, which was voluntary and not adopted). Instead, the U.S. approach is **“maximal innovation with minimal barriers, plus targeted safeguards.”** For example, in July 2023 the White House secured **voluntary commitments** from leading AI firms to do safety testing and share information with the government ([AI threatens humanity’s future, 61% of Americans say: Reuters/Ipsos poll | Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ai-threatens-humanitys-future-61-americans-say-reutersipsos-2023-05-17/#:~:text=,the%20need%20to%20take%20action)). In October 2023, the Executive Order on AI mandated some reporting to the government when very large models are trained ([Governance of superintelligence | OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/governance-of-superintelligence/#:~:text=First%2C%20we%20need%20some%20degree,a%20certain%20rate%20per%20year)), inching toward a licensing regime for frontier models, but it’s an executive action rather than legislation. Meanwhile, Congress in 2024–25 has been actively discussing AI regulations (multiple AI bills and a Senate AI Insight Forum were convened), but no comprehensive AI law has passed as of April 2025. Thus, **the regulatory posture in the US is still relatively light-touch** (especially compared to the EU), focusing on guidelines, standards (through NIST), and investment in AI safety research (like NSF grants, DARPA’s alignment projects).

The U.S. also promotes **AI alliances**: it works with allies on AI through the **G7 (the Hiroshima AI process)**, the **OECD’s AI Principles** (the U.S. joined those in 2019), and bilateral partnerships (e.g., the U.S.-EU Trade and Technology Council includes an AI working group to align standards). In a way, the U.S. tries to shape global norms in line with democratic values while ensuring its companies remain dominant. The U.S. often stresses openness and human rights in AI – for instance, it advocates for use of AI in humanitarian areas and has pushed back on authoritarian uses (like Chinese surveillance AI exports). However, there’s also a strong narrative of **“not letting China get ahead.”** This was evident when U.S. officials praised OpenAI/DeepMind achievements and simultaneously highlighted concerns about Chinese progress (like when China’s plan was announced, American analysts in 2017 warned of “Sputnik moment”). 

In summary, the U.S. environment is **competitive** (especially vis-à-vis China), but also **internally aware of safety**. U.S. AI companies often cooperate with the government (e.g., OpenAI’s Altman engaging with Congress, Frontier Model Forum coordinating with policy makers). The policy priority is maintaining a lead in AI R&D, securing supply chains (hence CHIPS Act funding for domestic semiconductor manufacturing), and developing *guardrails* (AI Bill of Rights blueprint, executive orders) to address societal risks. We can see the U.S. spearheading initiatives like an “AI Safety Institute” (the EO 2023 called for evaluating AI systems for biosecurity, etc.) and funding cutting-edge alignment research. Geopolitically, America is also likely to **integrate AI into defense**: the 2024 DoD strategy talks of “AI-enabled warfare” and working with allies like NATO on AI principles. This might not be AGI-specific, but it underscores the strategic milieu.

### China: State-Driven Ambition and Regulation

**China** views AI, including potential AGI, as a strategic sector in its rise as a superpower. The **New Generation AI Development Plan (2017)** explicitly aims for China to lead the world in AI by 2030 ([Putin says the nation that leads in AI ‘will be the ruler of the world’ | The Verge](https://www.theverge.com/2017/9/4/16251226/russia-ai-putin-rule-the-world#:~:text=The%20development%20of%20artificial%20intelligence,basic%20science%20and%20technology%20research)) ([Full Translation: China’s ‘New Generation Artificial Intelligence Development Plan’ (2017)](https://digichina.stanford.edu/work/full-translation-chinas-new-generation-artificial-intelligence-development-plan-2017/#:~:text=Third%2C%20by%202030%2C%20China%E2%80%99s%20AI,foundation%20for%20becoming%20a%20leading)). This ambition is backed by heavy government investment, subsidies, and national coordination. The Chinese government sees advanced AI as key to economic modernization, military strength, and even governance (e.g. “smart city” initiatives). As such, **China’s environment is one of mobilized national effort coupled with tight oversight**.

On the competition side, Chinese officials often speak of reducing dependence on Western tech – the U.S. export controls have further galvanized this. China has launched major programs to develop **indigenous AI chips** (e.g., Huawei, Alibaba and others are investing in high-end chips and networking to bypass U.S. GPUs). It has also invested in **talent**: encouraging Chinese AI PhDs abroad to return, and training many at home. By 2025, China reportedly has as many top AI researchers as the U.S. in some subfields, though in foundational model research the U.S. still leads in quality. Chinese companies like **Baidu, Tencent, Alibaba, Huawei, and smaller startups (e.g., SenseTime)** are actively developing large language models and multimodal systems. For instance, Baidu’s **ERNIE Bot** and Alibaba’s **Tongyi Qianwen** (both unveiled 2023) are Chinese analogs to ChatGPT, and more recently, reports of models with tens of billions of parameters (some approaching GPT-4 level, according to Chinese benchmark claims). While none publicly claim surpassing GPT-4 yet, progress is steady and state-supported. The government often touts these models to show China is not lagging.

However, **China’s approach to AI development is tempered by strict regulatory controls, especially on content and ethics in line with regime stability.** In August 2023, China implemented **comprehensive generative AI regulations** ([China: Generative AI Measures Finalized  | Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2023-07-18/china-generative-ai-measures-finalized/#:~:text=On%20July%2010%2C%202023%2C%20the,solicit%20comments%20from%20the%20public)) ([China: Generative AI Measures Finalized  | Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2023-07-18/china-generative-ai-measures-finalized/#:~:text=Article%206%20of%20the%20draft,the%20%2020%20Algorithmic%20Recommendation)). Providers of AI services must ensure content aligns with “core socialist values” and does not produce prohibited material. They must conduct security assessments and are required to register algorithms with authorities ([China: Generative AI Measures Finalized  | Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/global-legal-monitor/2023-07-18/china-generative-ai-measures-finalized/#:~:text=Article%206%20of%20the%20draft,the%20%2020%20Algorithmic%20Recommendation)). This means any advanced AGI in China would operate under heavy censorship and monitoring. In effect, China prioritizes **“controlled innovation”**: they want to lead in capability but not at the expense of political/social control. Another facet is **surveillance and law enforcement**: China employs AI widely for monitoring (facial recognition, predictive policing). This is encouraged and integrated into its governance, which contrasts with democratic concerns on privacy.

Geopolitically, China frames its AI pursuit as part of a multipolar tech world. It often calls out U.S. “tech hegemony” and advocates for **technology sovereignty** for nations. For instance, in international forums, China emphasizes that each country should have the right to develop AI and suggests principles of non-interference. At the same time, China *does* engage in some cooperative efforts: it participates in the UN AI discussions, sent delegates to the 2023 UK AI Safety Summit, and in 2021 joined the GPAI (Global Partnership on AI) as an observer after initially shunning it. But overall, China prefers **bilateral or its own multilateral venues** (like BRICS meetings on AI) over frameworks that might constrain it. One notable stance: China has been supportive of an idea of a global AI governance body under the UN, likely because it would have a strong say there.

On the **military front**, China’s PLA is investing in “AI-infused warfare” – swarms of drones, AI decision-support, etc. U.S. reports suggest China aims to use AI to leapfrog U.S. military advantages (e.g., through asymmetric capabilities). This adds a security layer to the AGI race: each side fears the other’s AI could provide strategic superiority, possibly even in AGI (some strategists worry an AGI could conceivably crack encryption, design novel weapons, etc.). This drives a subtle arms race dynamic: neither U.S. nor China wants to slow down too much for fear of losing edge.

**Chinese policy priorities** include self-reliance (the phrase “dual circulation” includes tech independence), AI ethics with Chinese characteristics (they put out principles too – in 2019 Beijing AI Principles talked about fairness and privacy, but enforcement is mainly content censorship), and becoming an exporter of AI technology. China already exports facial recognition and smart city systems to dozens of countries (sometimes termed the “AI Silk Road”), which extends its influence. In AGI context, if China were to achieve a major breakthrough, it might leverage it to bolster this tech influence abroad.

In summary, China is both a fierce **competitor in the AGI race**, pouring resources into catching up to U.S. capabilities, and a **strict regulator**, ensuring AI development doesn’t undermine regime interests. The balance they strike will affect how open Chinese AGI research is (currently, many Chinese researchers publish openly, but some advanced model details are kept proprietary). Notably, Chinese AI scientists are part of the global community; there is still cross-pollination (though reduced by visa and export restrictions lately). The environment is somewhat siloed – Chinese models for China’s internet, Western models for the Western internet – raising questions about how a globally generalized AGI would operate across these ecosystems.

### Europe: Regulatory Superpower and Human-Centric Approach

The **European Union** has taken on the role of the world’s **AI regulator and norm-setter**. Europe, lacking a tech giant presence in frontier AI (with some exceptions like DeepMind’s origins in the UK and several excellent research labs, but generally fewer industry-leading models), has focused on shaping the governance of AI. The hallmark is the **EU AI Act**, which was finalized in 2024 ([Long awaited EU AI Act becomes law after publication in the EU's ...](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/long-awaited-eu-ai-act-becomes-law-after-publication-eus-official-journal#:~:text=Long%20awaited%20EU%20AI%20Act,will%20commence%20on%202)). This law treats AI in a risk-based manner: it bans certain harmful uses (like social scoring), heavily regulates “high-risk” systems (e.g., in recruitment, credit, etc.), and imposes transparency on generative models (requiring disclosure of AI-generated content, for instance). For advanced general models, the AI Act doesn’t ban them but will likely classify them as high-risk, meaning developers must implement safety, transparency, and oversight measures before deployment. By pushing this legislation, the EU aims to enforce **accountability on AGI developers** – companies like OpenAI or Google will have to comply in the European market. The Act will fully apply by 2026 ([EU AI Act - Updates, Compliance, Training](https://www.artificial-intelligence-act.com/#:~:text=act,associated%20with%20the%20use)), but companies are already adapting.

Europe’s priority is **“Trustworthy AI”**, as outlined in its 2019 ethics guidelines and subsequent policies. This means any pursuit of AGI should align with European values of privacy, human dignity, and democracy. European officials have explicitly mentioned superintelligence in policy discussions – for example, in 2023 some Members of European Parliament suggested that extremely advanced AI (AGI-level) might even need a global treaty, akin to WMDs. Europe is also concerned about **AI’s impact on jobs and social welfare**, hence emphasizing retraining and education in its AI strategies.

Geopolitically, the EU doesn’t frame AI as an arms race like the U.S. and China, but rather as something that requires **international cooperation and regulation**. European leaders (e.g., French President Macron, EU Commission President von der Leyen) have called for global agreements on AI safety. The EU has been engaging the U.S. in aligning regulatory approaches (though the U.S. is more laissez-faire). Also, Europe engages China on AI ethics – China co-led a UNESCO recommendation on AI ethics that was adopted by many countries in 2021, showing some cooperative willingness in principle.

One unique aspect is **UK vs EU**: Post-Brexit UK is carving its own AI path. The **UK**, no longer bound by the EU AI Act, positioned itself as a hub for AI safety and innovation. The UK’s approach (as of 2023 White Paper) is a lighter regulatory touch than the EU, focusing on guiding principles enforced by existing regulators rather than new hard law. The UK hosted the first AI Safety Summit, showing leadership in convening discussions on frontier AI. It also announced an **AI Safety Institute** to research frontier model risks. So the UK is trying to straddle being pro-innovation (not scaring off OpenAI/DeepMind, which operate heavily in UK) and pro-safety (leading global discourse). Tension appeared in 2025 when UK and US didn’t sign the Paris Summit communique, possibly because they found it too constraining ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=On%20February%2011%2C%20heads%20of,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground)) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=contrast%20to%20the%20first%20summit,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground)). The **EU** and countries like France, on the other hand, champion a more interventionist approach (the “third way” mentioned in the Atlantic Council piece ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=the%20United%20States%20and%20the,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground)) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=intelligence%20%28AI%29,and%20rooted%20in%20shared%20prosperity)) – strategic independence through open-source, etc., aligned with Global South).

Thus, Europe is something of a **referee** in the AGI space: not the top player but trying to set the rules. It prioritizes issues like safety, ethics, and *human-centric AI*. For example, the EU has funded projects on explainable AI, and it’s likely to fund AGI safety research as well (the Horizon Europe research program includes calls for work on AI alignment). European public opinion, as noted, gives political cover for this strict approach – Europeans generally approve of regulating AI strongly.

### Global South and Developing Countries: Engagement and Calls for Inclusion

Countries in the **Global South** (which includes emerging economies and developing nations across Asia, Africa, Latin America) have diverse stances, but a common theme is they **do not want to be mere spectators in the AI revolution**. There is a clear understanding that AI (and eventually AGI) could either help solve developmental challenges or widen global inequalities. Thus, many developing countries are crafting their own AI strategies and calling for a voice in global AI governance.

**India** stands out as a major player: as of 2025, India has a fast-growing tech sector and government initiatives like “AI for All.” India’s approach emphasizes using AI for social development (e.g., agriculture, healthcare) and being an “AI garage” for solutions that can be exported to other developing nations. Geopolitically, India aligns somewhat with Western democracies on AI ethics, but also advocates for the interests of the Global South. At the Paris 2025 Summit, India’s co-chair role signaled it is bridging between West and developing world ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=What%20this%20has%20resulted%20in,and%20rooted%20in%20shared%20prosperity)) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=intelligence%20%28AI%29,and%20rooted%20in%20shared%20prosperity)). India’s Digital Minister has spoken about ensuring **equitable access to AI and avoiding a new tech divide**. India also is cautious about over-regulation domestically – it did not impose heavy AI regulations yet, leaning towards innovation-friendly policies, but it does support international guardrails for extreme cases (PM Modi in 2023 said we need basic rules-of-the-road for AI globally).

**Other Asia (ASEAN, etc.)**: Countries like Singapore, South Korea (though SK is developed), UAE (Gulf) – these are investing heavily in AI, aiming to be regional leaders. They often focus on capacity-building (training AI talent, attracting AI companies). For instance, the UAE appointed a Minister for AI and released a national AI strategy early (2017), focusing on government adoption. Many of these countries participate in global forums and generally support cooperative governance, but also want to ensure regulations don’t prevent them from catching up. **Singapore** often acts as a neutral convener and has published model AI governance frameworks that balance innovation and oversight, which can influence others.

**Africa and Latin America**: The African Union in 2023 was working on a **Continental AI Strategy**, recognizing both the potential benefits in sectors like agriculture and the need for African values (like community benefit, avoiding bias against African populations in AI systems). African nations often lack the computational resources for frontier model development, so they emphasize partnerships and open access. One key ask from African and Latin American delegations in global talks is **technology transfer and capacity building** – they want AI not to be dominated by a few rich nations. This has led to proposals like a global fund for AI for development, and inclusion of developing experts in bodies like the Global Partnership on AI. 

The **Global South collectively is pushing for inclusion in governance**: At the UN, developing countries have suggested that any “AI governance regime” must give them representation, so rules aren’t just written by US/EU/China. For example, in late 2024 at the UN General Assembly, some African and Latin American states supported the idea of a **“Global AI Observatory” under UN auspices**, where all countries contribute data on AI progress. They are also wary of overly restrictive global rules that could entrench the lead of current AI powers – for instance, a moratorium on certain research might lock in a hierarchy with the US and China on top.

We see emerging alliances like the **“Global South AI caucus”** informally – India, Brazil, South Africa and others aligning on points like algorithmic fairness, localization (ensuring AI speaks their languages, understands local context). There is also cooperation via groups like BRICS, which discussed AI in summits, and via South-South partnerships (for example, African researchers collaborating with Indian or Latin American institutes on AI projects geared to local needs).

From a policy priority perspective: **Global South countries prioritize AI for economic development, education, healthcare, and agriculture**, and they want international support for those applications. They are less interested in AGI arms-race and more in catching up on basic AI adoption. However, they also don’t want to wake up one day to an AGI controlled by someone else. So there are calls (notably from some Latin American voices at the UN) for an **international AGI oversight body where all countries have a seat** – akin to how all countries engage on climate change governance, for example. 

Some in the Global South also raise the issue of **data governance** – since AI training feeds on data, largely from Western sources, there’s an argument that developing countries’ data should be protected or used to benefit them. This is part of digital sovereignty claims (e.g., some countries requiring that their citizen data used to train AI be stored locally or subject to local laws).

### International Cooperation vs. Competition

While competition is intense (especially U.S.-China), the global community has recognized that **uncontrolled development of AGI could pose existential risks or at least global externalities that no single nation can manage**. This has led to nascent cooperative frameworks:

- The **Bletchley Park Summit (UK 2023)** created a *Global Frontier AI Taskforce* – initially a pledge to share information on evaluating the most advanced models. The U.S., EU, and China all signed on to general principles of risk assessment ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=On%20February%2011%2C%20heads%20of,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground)). However, these are non-binding. By the 2025 Paris Summit, consensus frayed a bit (US/UK wanting more freedom, others wanting more binding rules) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=On%20February%2011%2C%20heads%20of,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground)) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=the%20United%20States%20and%20the,technological%20sovereignty%20has%20gained%20ground)).

- The **Global Partnership on AI (GPAI)**, launched in 2020 by OECD countries plus India and others, is an important forum. It’s not about AGI specifically, but it has working groups on responsible AI that indirectly cover advanced AI. It allows for multistakeholder input (including experts from Global South). GPAI’s annual report in 2024 included a section on “future of AI” calling for research cooperation on safety and robustness of increasingly general AI.

- The **United Nations**: In 2023 the UN Secretary-General proposed establishing a high-level advisory board for AI; by 2024 it was set up and included experts from around the world (including China, the US, Europe, and Africa). The UN is a platform where developing nations insist on being heard, and it may eventually host some treaty or at least guiding principles. UNESCO’s Recommendation on AI Ethics (adopted by 193 countries in Nov 2021) gave a broad framework (it emphasizes human rights, fairness, and peace). While UNESCO’s is non-binding, it’s notable that even China and the US (which are usually at odds) agreed to that text. This shows there is *some common ground* on high-level principles.

- **Bilateral cooperation**: Despite rivalry, there have been quiet talks between the U.S. and China on AI safety – e.g., scientific collaborations on AI and climate modeling, or discussions under arms control auspices about lethal autonomous weapons. These are parallels to Cold War-era science diplomacy. Though currently limited, such engagements may increase as AGI nears reality (neither side wants an accidental catastrophe). There’s also track-II dialogues (unofficial expert meetings) between Chinese and American AI scientists talking about norms (supported by think tanks).

- **Private sector and civil society** also act transnationally: Many top AI researchers (e.g., at conferences like NeurIPS, IJCAI) are collaboratively developing best practices. Organizations like the **IEEE and ISO** are creating global technical standards for AI (covering safety, reliability, transparency). For example, IEEE’s Ethically Aligned Design initiative has global input. Such standards could indirectly shape how AGI is designed.

One notable tension: **Open-source vs proprietary**. Some argue open collaboration on AGI (as advocated by certain Global South and European actors) could democratize benefits and reduce single-point failure risk. The other side (some in US, and companies) worry open-sourcing very advanced models could increase misuse risk or lose competitive edge. This was a theme at the 2025 Paris Summit, where open-source commitments were seen as a way to include more countries ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=What%20this%20has%20resulted%20in,and%20rooted%20in%20shared%20prosperity)) ([At the Paris AI Action Summit, the Global South rises - Atlantic Council](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/at-the-paris-ai-action-summit-the-global-south-rises/#:~:text=intelligence%20%28AI%29,and%20rooted%20in%20shared%20prosperity)).

In terms of regional priorities as of 2025:
- **U.S.:** Winning the tech race, securing AI supply chains, fostering innovation, and gradually implementing guardrails to prevent disasters (with industry self-regulation in the interim).
- **China:** Catching up/leading in AI under state guidance, using AI to bolster the regime and economic growth, controlling information and societal impact tightly, and resisting Western dominance in setting AI rules.
- **EU:** Imposing accountability on AI developers, protecting citizens from harms, and diplomatically pushing for international governance (with itself at the forefront normatively).
- **Global South:** Leveraging AI for development, ensuring they are not excluded from benefits or rule-making, seeking collaborative development (often with open tech) and capacity building, and voicing concern about inequality and bias.

A specific geopolitical flashpoint is AI in military – an arms control regime for AI is so far absent. In 2024, some at the UN proposed a ban on autonomous lethal weapons, but major powers didn’t agree to a ban (preferring softer “principles”). If AGI is seen as potentially yielding decisive military advantage, the lack of trust between great powers could impede full cooperation. However, the existential risk angle (AGI as a potential uncontrollable entity) could push even rivals to cooperate akin to how nations cooperate on asteroid defense – a threat above politics. Indeed, scientists from U.S., China, Russia penned joint letters in 2024 urging world leaders to treat AGI safety as a global public good.

**In conclusion**, the global environment for AGI development is one of **competitive sprinting within a gradually emerging safety net**. Great power rivalry (U.S.-China) provides huge resources to develop AGI, but also raises risk of conflict or secrecy. Meanwhile, middle powers and global institutions work to put in place norms and checklists to ensure this technology, when it arrives, is beneficial and shared. The next few years (2025–2030) will likely determine if humanity handles AGI in a cooperative manner or a fragmented one. As of April 2025, **the trajectory is cautiously optimistic**: key players publicly acknowledge the need for coordination (e.g., OpenAI calling for an IAEA-like body ([Governance of superintelligence | OpenAI](https://openai.com/index/governance-of-superintelligence/#:~:text=of%20acting%20responsibly)), DeepMind calling for a UN body ([AI Could Achieve Human-Like Intelligence By 2030 And 'Destroy Mankind', Google Predicts](https://www.ndtv.com/science/ai-could-achieve-human-like-intelligence-by-2030-and-destroy-mankind-google-predicts-8105066#:~:text=Demis%20Hassabis%2C%20CEO%20of%20DeepMind%2C,organisation%20to%20oversee%20AGI%27s%20development))), initial forums have been held, and even adversaries are talking. But concrete mechanisms are still in infancy. The policies being set now – export controls, the EU Act, bilateral agreements – will shape who gets to develop AGI and under what rules. The world’s regions are aligned in recognizing AI’s importance but **diverse in strategy**: the U.S. and China prioritize leadership, Europe prioritizes regulation, and the Global South prioritizes equitable access. The ultimate outcome for superintelligence will depend on navigating these differences to craft governance that is globally inclusive and effective – a challenge unprecedented in the history of technology governance.
