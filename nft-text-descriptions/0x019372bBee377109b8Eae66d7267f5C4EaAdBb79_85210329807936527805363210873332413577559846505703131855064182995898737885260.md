Event Overview:

On January 13, 2025, today,the White House released a landmark "Fact Sheet" titled "Ensuring U.S. Security and Economic Strength in the Age of Artificial Intelligence," outlining an Interim Final Rule on Artificial Intelligence Diffusion. This pivotal document marks a critical turning point in global AI governance, as the U.S. government takes decisive steps to shape the development and deployment of this transformative technology. The rule, with its six key mechanisms, aims to bolster U.S. national security and economic strength by promoting the responsible diffusion of American AI technology while strategically controlling the export and use of advanced AI systems. This includes flexibility for allies and partners, streamlined processing of large chip orders for trusted entities, and restrictions on the transfer of advanced closed-weight models to untrusted actors. The release of this rule comes amidst intensifying global competition in the AI field and the looming advent of the Artificial Superintelligence (ASI) era. The U.S. seeks to solidify its leadership in AI while building a global AI ecosystem aligned with its values.

Technological and Policy Significance:

The Interim Final Rule represents a proactive approach by the U.S. government to manage the proliferation of AI technology, reflecting a strategic consideration of innovation, economic interests, and national security. By establishing a tiered control system, the U.S. aims to:

Maintain its leadership in the field of artificial intelligence: Particularly in high-end chips and advanced models.

Prevent key AI technologies from falling into the hands of adversarial nations or entities.

Establish a trust-based AI ecosystem with allies and partners.

Promote the responsible development and deployment of AI technologies.

Six Key Mechanisms:

Allied Exemption: No restrictions apply to chip sales to 18 key allies and partners, enabling jurisdictions with robust technology protection regimes and technology ecosystems aligned with U.S. national security and foreign policy interests to benefit from seamless large-scale purchases.

Streamlined Chip Order Processing: Chip orders with collective computation power up to roughly 1,700 advanced GPUs do not require a license and do not count against national chip caps. This simplifies the processing of the majority of chip orders, rapidly accelerating low-risk shipments of U.S. technology around the world.

Universal Verified End User (UVEU) Status: Entities that meet high security and trust standards and are headquartered in close allies and partners can obtain highly trusted “Universal Verified End User” (UVEU) status. With this status, they can then place up to 7% of their global AI computational capacity in countries around the world – likely amounting to hundreds of thousands of chips. This trusted status is granted on a global and enduring basis, allowing responsible entities to expand rapidly and flexibly, and strengthening U.S. and allied global leadership while keeping frontier training at home.

National Verified End User (NVEU) Status: Entities that meet the same security requirements and are headquartered in any destination that is not a country of concern can apply for “National Verified End User” status, enabling them to purchase computational power equivalent to up to 320,000 advanced GPUs over the next two years. This provision permits trusted national entities to benefit from advanced U.S. technology, serving local, governmental, and regional customers, while guarding against diversion risks.

Non-VEU Entity Limits: Non-VEU entities located outside of close allies can still purchase large amounts of computational power, up to the equivalent of 50,000 advanced GPUs per country. This cap ensures that U.S. technology is available to service foreign governments, healthcare providers, and other local businesses.

Government-to-Government Arrangements: Governments that sign arrangements aligning their export control, clean energy, and technology security efforts with the United States can double their chip caps (up to 100,000 of today’s advanced GPUs).

Restrictions on Untrusted Actors:

Ensuring that advanced semiconductors sold abroad are not used by countries of concern to train advanced AI systems, while still permitting access for general-purpose applications from telecommunications to banking.

Restricting the transfer to non-trusted actors of the model weights for advanced closed-weight models. The rule does not in any way inhibit the publication of model weights for open-weight models.

Setting security standards to protect the weights of advanced closed-weight AI models, permitting them to be stored and used securely around the world while helping prevent illicit adversary access.
